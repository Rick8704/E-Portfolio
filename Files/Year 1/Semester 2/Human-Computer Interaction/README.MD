## 📖 What I Learned

Human-Computer Interaction shifted my perspective from "how does the system work?" to "how does the user experience the system?". I learned usability principles, interface design heuristics, and methods for evaluating user experience — including task analysis, prototyping, and user testing frameworks.

The course showed that good design is invisible — users should be able to accomplish their goals without thinking about the interface. Poor design creates friction, confusion, and frustration that can undermine even technically excellent systems.

---

## 💭 Reflection

**What I gained:** A user-centred mindset that I apply to every interface I design or evaluate. Understanding Nielsen's usability heuristics gave me a reliable framework for critiquing and improving designs.

**Suggestions for improvement:** Conducting actual usability tests with real users — even informally — would have made the theoretical principles much more tangible. Observing how someone struggles with an interface teaches more than reading about usability failures.

**Other thoughts:** HCI has had a quiet but significant influence on my work. Whenever I build a data dashboard, application, or report, I now think about who will use it and what they need to accomplish — not just whether the system produces correct output.
